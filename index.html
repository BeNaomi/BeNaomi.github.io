About

I am a PhD Candidate in Management Information Systems at the UBC Sauder School of Business. My research aims to answer the question: How can we efficiently design AI systems that support users with conflicting interests?

I study these questions in public-facing settings, where AI systems provide personalized recommendations but outcomes are shared. In these environments, people are evaluating the system and navigating a tension between their own interests and the collective good. My work examines how explainable AI (XAI) shapes this tension, interacts with fairness judgments, and ultimately affects whether users choose to engage with AI-driven systems. I also explore how XAI can be used to better govern AI systems in resource-allocation contexts and ensure that policymakers achieve the most efficient and just outcome.

Before transitioning to academia, I worked as legal counsel at the Ministry of Finance. I was involved in regulatory decisions, the allocation of public resources, policy implementation, and efforts to address resistance from affected groups. I saw firsthand how even well-designed policies can reshape behavior towards a better future, or fail without public cooperation.

That realization continues to shape my research. I am interested in how AI systems can support fair, transparent, and efficient decision-making, especially in the public systems that shape everyday life.

Research Agenda

I examine how different forms of XAI affect users’ behavior when AI recommendations are interdependent

Fairness, Justice, and Cooperation

My work studies how fairness judgments shape cooperation with AI-enabled public systems, especially under uncertainty and competing interests.

AI Governance and Verification

I am developing work on how explainability tools such as SHAP can support fairness governance and sociotechnical verification.
